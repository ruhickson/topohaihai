Wave Race 64
============

Description
-----------
Jet ski racing on water with realistic-ish wave physics and buoy-marked courses. Players unlock harder classes and stunt opportunities; known for water effects that showed off early N64 capabilities.

Genre: Racing (watercraft / jet ski)

Developer: Nintendo EAD (Nintendo Software Technology — NST — involvement appears in some credits for localization/support)

Publisher: Nintendo

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)
----------------------
• Japan: September 27, 1996  
• North America: November 1, 1996  
• Europe: April 1997 (often cited April 29, 1997)  

Ratings / certification
-------------------------
• ESRB: E (Everyone) — US  

Other notes
-----------
• Sequel *Wave Race: Blue Storm* on GameCube.  
• Nintendo Switch Online + Expansion Pack includes N64 re-release in some regions.

Thoughts
--------
Wave Race 64 is the game that made water feel like a character—waves shoving you around, wakes messing with your line, and that smug satisfaction when you nail a buoy turn anyway. Highlights: handling is deep for an arcade racer; stunt mode is surprisingly fun; the vibe is sunny and competitive at the same time. Lowlights: learning curve is real—early races feel fine, later ones punish tiny mistakes, and your friends will hear you swear at digital jet skis.

I love how it showcases the N64 without needing a huge world: it’s just water, sky, and speed, but the physics sell the fantasy. Multiplayer is chaotic in the best way—splashing, bumping, laughing. Single-player can feel short if you’re blazing through, but mastery is the replay hook. Also, the announcer energy is permanently stuck in my head.

Stunt mode is the secret sauce for me: it’s not just racing, it’s showing off, and it turns the water into a playground. I’m also a sucker for how the courses change feel—sunset vibes, choppy weather, that sense that you’re in a place rather than a flat map.

Difficulty ramps hard in later classes—what felt breezy becomes “okay, I respect water now,” and that’s when the game stops being a toy and starts being a sport.

The water physics still feel special because they’re not just visual—they change how you approach every turn, jump, and landing.

I love the little audio stingers when you nail a buoy turn—pure chef’s kiss feedback, like the game is proud of you for once.

Rain choppiness and sunset glare aren’t just cosmetic—they actually change how aggressive you can be on a turn, which is pretty slick for the era. Little details like that are why I still boot it.

Final thought: two thumbs up—tight, stylish, and still one of Nintendo’s coolest “show off the hardware” racers.

**Verdict:** 👍👍 (two thumbs up)
