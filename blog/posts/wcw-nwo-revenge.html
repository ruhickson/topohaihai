WCW/nWo Revenge

Description

Wrestling game sequel to WCW vs. nWo: World Tour using AKI’s acclaimed grappling engine. Large roster of WCW/nWo talent, multiple match types, and four-player support. Often considered one of the best wrestling games on N64.

Genre: Sports (professional wrestling)

Developer: Asmik Ace Entertainment / AKI Corporation (synonymous with AKI Engine era titles; published under THQ in West)

Publisher: THQ

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)

• North America: November 10, 1998
• Europe: typically 1998–1999 (often cited early 1999 for some PAL territories)

Ratings / certification

• ESRB: E (Everyone) — US

Other notes

• Successor WWF WrestleMania 2000 uses the same engine lineage.
• Verify exact EU date on your PAL box—THQ EU distribution dates can vary by country.

Thoughts

WCW/nWo Revenge is the wrestling game I think of when someone says “N64 grappling” in a happy voice. The AKI engine feels weighty and readable—locks up, counters, big moves—so matches tell stories even when you’re just messing around with friends. Highlights: huge roster for the time, four-player matches are pure chaos, and create-a-wrestler adds longevity if you’re into crafting weird dudes with bad outfits. Lowlights: it’s dated visually (which I like, but still), AI can be weird, and if you don’t care about wrestling at all, it’s not magically converting you.

What sells it is the couch drama: alliances, betrayals, someone spamming the same move until the room bans it. It’s also a time capsule of late-90s wrestling culture—nWo vibes everywhere. Compared to modern wrestling sims, it’s simpler, but the simplicity keeps matches fast.

Create-a-wrestler adds longevity if you’re into crafting weird champions and running them through the gauntlet. I’m not claiming it’s deep simulation, but it’s deep fun—the kind where you remember the storylines you invented. Reversals and counters feel readable, which is why matches can turn on a dime without feeling random.

Tag matches and battle royals are where the chaos multiplier goes exponential—if you thought one-on-one was dramatic, wait until four humans are all trying to cheat at once.

Entrance themes and roster energy are pure late-90s wrestling—if you know the names, you’ll grin like an idiot when the bell rings.

Costumes and arenas are simple, but the animation sells the hits—when a big move lands, the whole room reacts like it’s pay-per-view night.

Even losing can be funny—someone always tries a dumb pin escape and pays for it with pride.

You’ll remember the rivalries you invented more than whatever the game’s “story” technically is—that’s the good stuff. Final thought: two thumbs up—if you like wrestling even a little, this is essential N64.

Verdict: 👍👍 (two thumbs up)
