Pokémon Snap

Description

On-rails photography game set on Pokémon Island: ride through courses and take pictures of wild Pokémon for Professor Oak’s research. Scoring rewards pose, size, and action shots; unlock items to open new routes and encounters.

Genre: Rail shooter / photography (non-violent)

Developer: HAL Laboratory, Pax Softnica (credited on some releases)

Publisher: Nintendo

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)

• Japan: March 21, 1999
• North America: June 30, 1999
• Europe: September 15, 1999

Ratings / certification

• ESRB: E (Everyone) — US

Other notes

• New Pokémon Snap (Switch, 2021) is a modern follow-up.
• Blockbuster stores in the US sometimes offered sticker printing kiosks for in-game photos (era-specific promotion).

Thoughts

Pokémon Snap is basically a theme park ride where your job is to be annoying with a camera, and I mean that lovingly. You’re on rails, you’re trying to make Pikachu look good, and you’re constantly realizing you missed the perfect shot because you blinked. Highlights: it’s relaxing in a way that almost no other Pokémon game is—no grinding levels, no faint spirals, just vibes and composition. The unlock progression (apples, music, etc.) turns each course into a little toybox to experiment with. Lowlights: it’s short if you’re efficient, and replaying the same track for a better score can feel repetitive if you’re not into the photography loop.

What I adore is how it trains you to see Pokémon as characters: expressions, interactions, silly moments you’d never notice in a battle screen. It’s also a nostalgia factory—if you grew up with Gen 1, this is basically a hug. The downside is modern players might find it thin without the nostalgia buffer, and the N64 visuals are chunky (though I think that adds charm).

The scoring loop is sneakily addictive: you’re not just snapping—you’re hunting drama. A fight between two Pokémon, a perfectly timed evolve moment, Pikachu on a surfboard like it’s auditioning for a commercial—those are the shots you brag about. Apple tossing becomes second nature, and then you’re optimizing routes like a speedrunner with a camera.

Course unlocks reward curiosity without turning the whole thing into a checklist from hell—though completionists will still make spreadsheets because that’s who we are.

Professor Oak judging your photos is basically a roast session with science layered on top, and chasing high scores turns chill rides into obsessive studio sessions.

Replay runs become this weird mix of art direction and speedrunning—same track, new angles, new drama. Final thought: two thumbs up—small game, huge heart, and weirdly timeless as a chill-out experience.

Verdict: 👍👍 (two thumbs up)
