Perfect Dark

Description

Spiritual successor to GoldenEye 007: sci-fi FPS starring Joanna Dark, with campaign missions, co-op/bot-supported modes (via Expansion Pak in many cases), and extensive multiplayer options. Noted for weapons, simulants, and mission objectives.

Genre: First-person shooter

Developer: Rare

Publisher: Rare (NA)

Platform: Nintendo 64
Requires: Expansion Pak for most single-player campaign and many features (as stated on packaging)

Release dates (retail)

• North America: May 22, 2000
• Europe: June 30, 2000
• Japan: October 21, 2000

Ratings / certification

• ESRB: M (Mature) — US (blood/gore in options; violence)

Other notes

• Perfect Dark XBLA (2010) remaster for Xbox 360.
• Nintendo 64 cartridge is typically 64MB (512-megabit) — among the largest N64 carts.

Thoughts

Perfect Dark is what happens when you take GoldenEye’s DNA and crank the sci-fi spy nonsense up until the cartridge starts sweating. Joanna Dark is cool, the gadgets are fun, and the missions have more layers than “shoot the room” (though there’s plenty of shooting too). Highlights: AI bots in multiplayer are a whole hobby in themselves, and the weapon variety is ridiculous in the best way—laptop guns, tranquilizers, whatever chaos you’re in the mood for. Lowlights: you basically need the Expansion Pak for the real campaign experience, and the framerate can absolutely fall apart when the action gets heavy, which is both funny and cursed.

I think the campaign is underrated compared to multiplayer, but it’s still a bit uneven—some missions are masterpieces of objectives and pacing, others feel like they’re testing your patience. The story goes places, sometimes silly places, but it commits. Co-op is a blast if you can set it up, and Combat Simulator remains a “one more match” machine. Compared to modern shooters, yeah, it’s clunky—but the sandboxy feel of the weapons and modes makes up for a lot.

Joanna’s gadgets and mission objectives give the campaign a spy-thriller texture that GoldenEye only touched sometimes—more sci-fi, more “what if Bond had aliens and drones.” The downside is that ambition strains the N64: busy scenes chug, and aiming is a skill you have to relearn if you’re used to twin sticks. I don’t mind, because the payoff is experimentation: try a weird loadout, spawn weird bots, make your own chaos.

Combat Simulator is basically a toybox: simulants, challenges, weird rule sets—if you’re creative, you’ll find new ways to break your friends’ spirits. That’s where Perfect Dark lives longest after the campaign credits roll. Final thought: two thumbs up for me; it’s a messy, ambitious classic that still feels like a flex for Rare on N64.

Verdict: 👍👍 (two thumbs up)
