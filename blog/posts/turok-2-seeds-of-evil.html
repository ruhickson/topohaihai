Turok 2: Seeds of Evil

Description

FPS sequel with larger levels, more weapons, and objectives; players fight aliens and creatures across the Lost Land. Notable for visual effects (fog, particle work for N64) and multiplayer modes. Requires Expansion Pak for full single-player campaign on N64.

Genre: First-person shooter

Developer: Iguana Entertainment

Publisher: Acclaim Entertainment

Platform: Nintendo 64
Requires: Expansion Pak (standard for main campaign experience—check packaging)

Release dates (retail)

• North America: December 10, 1998
• Europe: typically 1998–1999 (staggered by region)
• Japan: October 1, 1999

Ratings / certification

• ESRB: M (Mature) — US

Other notes

• Remastered Turok 2: Seeds of Evil released on modern consoles (Nightdive Studios).
• Expansion Pak bundle editions were common.

Thoughts

Turok 2 is what happens when Turok gets bigger: bigger levels, bigger weapons, bigger “wait where am I supposed to go?” energy. Highlights: the arsenal is ridiculous in a fun way—names and effects feel like a teenager’s sketchbook, and co-op/multiplayer adds longevity if you have the setup. The Expansion Pak requirement basically tells you the game was pushing the hardware hard. Lowlights: performance can chug, objectives can feel cryptic, and backtracking can drag if you’re not vibing with exploration.

I respect the ambition more than I enjoy every minute—some sections are brilliant, others feel like a maze with teeth. Still, when it clicks, it feels like a chunky sci-fi epic with dinosaurs and attitude.

Weapons are the star: the Cerebral Bore is infamous for a reason, and the game leans into spectacle over subtlety. The Expansion Pak requirement is a flex and a barrier—if you didn’t have it as a kid, you might have had a weird half-experience. I think the remaster on modern hardware proves the design had legs; the N64 version is the “authentic” way to suffer beautifully.

Co-op and multiplayer add longevity if you can wrangle the setup—campaign is huge, and playing with a buddy turns the maze-like levels into a coordinated rescue mission (or a blame game).

Objectives sometimes ask you to backtrack through hostile soup, which is where the game tests your patience more than your aim—bring a map mentality, not a sprint mentality.

When performance dips, it’s annoying—but when it holds steady, the scale feels huge for a cartridge shooter, like you’re actually trekking through something hostile and weird.

Boss fights are big, loud, and a little janky—exactly what you want from a Turok sequel that’s trying to outdo the first game’s spectacle. Final thought: two thumbs up for fans of old-school shooters with big maps; it’s messy, but it’s memorable.

Verdict: 👍👍 (two thumbs up)
