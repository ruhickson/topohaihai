The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time

Description

Landmark 3D action-adventure: Link travels through time (young/adult) to stop Ganondorf, clear dungeons, solve puzzles, and explore Hyrule. Introduced Z-targeting and set a template for 3D Zelda games. Frequently listed among the greatest games of all time.

Genre: Action-adventure

Developer: Nintendo EAD

Publisher: Nintendo

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)

• Japan: November 21, 1998
• North America: November 23, 1998
• Europe/Australia: December 11, 1998

Ratings / certification

• ESRB: E (Everyone) — US (original)

Other notes

• Master Quest and later ports appeared on GameCube and 3DS.
• iQue Player release in China (2003) exists for regional hardware.

Thoughts

Ocarina of Time is the game I measure other adventure games against, unfairly, because it basically raised me. The first time you step out into Hyrule Field and the music swells, it’s not “good for its time”—it’s just good, full stop. Dungeons are puzzles with personality: Forest Temple vibes, Water Temple complaints (yeah, I said it), Spirit Temple swagger—each one teaches you something new about your toolkit. Highlights: Z-targeting changed how 3D combat felt on a controller, and the story beats still land with surprising sincerity for a game with a talking tree. Lowlights: N64 textures are a soup sometimes, and revisiting Hyrule Field can feel empty compared to modern open worlds, even though at the time it felt enormous.

What still gets me is the pacing between “cozy village kid” and “the world is ending,” and how the game earns that shift. Side stuff like fishing and mini-games gives you breathing room so the epic doesn’t feel like a lecture. That said, some items are basically one-trick ponies you’ll barely touch after a dungeon, which is classic Zelda design but still a little silly in hindsight. Also, Navi’s “hey listen” energy is a meme for a reason—helpful, then immediately not helpful. I replay it every few years like checking in on an old friend: some parts aged, some parts didn’t, but the heart of it is still there.

Dungeon-by-dungeon, the game teaches you a language of puzzles: fire, water, time, spirit—each temple remixes ideas you thought you understood. Boss fights are more theatrical than mechanically complex by modern standards, but they stick in memory because the music and framing sell the moment. Hyrule Field’s emptiness reads differently now than it did in 1998, yet it still feels like freedom rather than void—especially when Epona’s gallop kicks in. Final thought: two thumbs up, obviously—it's a landmark for a reason, warts and all.

Verdict: 👍👍 (two thumbs up)
