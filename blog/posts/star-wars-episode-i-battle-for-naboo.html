Star Wars Episode I: Battle for Naboo

Description

Vehicle combat game set during The Phantom Menace: pilot Naboo starfighters, speeders, and other craft across missions loosely tied to the film’s battles. From the Rogue Squadron team’s lineage (Factor 5), with mission structure and unlockable craft.

Genre: Action / vehicle combat (third-person)

Developer: Factor 5

Publisher: LucasArts; THQ distributed in PAL regions on some packaging

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)

• North America: December 18, 2000
• Europe: typically March 2001 (often cited March 30, 2001)

Ratings / certification

• ESRB: T (Teen) — US

Other notes

• PC version also exists; N64 was a late-cycle release on the platform.
• Uses Expansion Pak for enhanced graphics mode on N64 (as indicated on packaging).

Thoughts

Battle for Naboo is basically Rogue Squadron’s younger sibling that really wants you to know it watched The Phantom Menace a few too many times—in a fun way, mostly. Highlights: flying missions with Star Wars sound design still hits, blowing up targets feels arcade-clean, and if you liked Factor 5’s handling model, this is more of that flavor. Lowlights: it’s not as iconic as Rogue Squadron overall, some missions get repetitive (“another escort, another turret”), and late N64 expectations mean you’re not getting modern flight-sim depth.

I think it’s a solid “Saturday afternoon” game: you boot it up, do a couple missions, feel like a kid with a toy Naboo starfighter, then put it down. The story isn’t going to blow your mind unless you’re deeply invested in Episode I lore, but it doesn’t need to—it’s a vehicle fantasy. Expansion Pak stuff is nice if you have the hardware; if not, you’re still okay.

Mission variety is the make-or-break: when you’re doing satisfying strafing runs and blowing up ground targets, it feels like Star Wars. When you’re chasing an objective marker through fog and confusion, it feels like work. I think it’s best enjoyed in short bursts rather than a marathon binge—especially on N64 where comfort breaks matter.

If you’re a Factor 5 fan coming from Rogue Squadron, your brain will recognize the “house style” even when the mission design isn’t as consistently tight. That familiarity helps a lot when the hardware limitations poke through.

Late N64 releases sometimes feel like they’re squeezing blood from a stone—this is impressive technically, but you can feel the console straining when the action gets busy.

If you treat it like an arcade flight toy rather than a deep sim, you’ll have more fun—try to “live here” and you’ll notice the seams faster. Final thought: one thumb up—good vibes, not a masterpiece, but a fun ride for Star Wars flight fans.

Verdict: 👍 (one thumb up)
