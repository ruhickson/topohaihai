Quake (Quake 64)
================

Description
-----------
Port of id Software’s seminal dark-fantasy/sci-fi FPS *Quake* to Nintendo 64, with single-player levels, deathmatch, and a cartridge-appropriate soundtrack/graphics compromise vs PC. Multiplayer split-screen supported.

Genre: First-person shooter

Developer: id Software (original); N64 port development often credited to Midway Games San Diego / Midway for conversion work (check specific credits on your release)

Publisher: Midway Games (North America); GT Interactive / Midway branding varies by region

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)
----------------------
• North America: March 24, 1998  
• Europe: typically May 1998 (often cited May 15, 1998)  

Ratings / certification
-------------------------
• ESRB: M (Mature) — US  

Other notes
-----------
• Distinct from PC version in level selection and audio presentation.  
• Often grouped with *Doom 64* as key N64 FPS ports of id-era shooters.

Thoughts
--------
Quake on N64 is a weird little artifact: it’s Quake, but it’s also *not* the PC experience you remember if you were playing with a mouse and a CRT at LAN parties. Highlights: it still has that gritty, industrial horror vibe, and playing an id shooter with a split-screen buddy on a couch is a specific flavor of fun that PC doesn’t replicate. Lowlights: controls are always going to be a debate—if you didn’t grow up with N64 FPS aiming, it can feel swimmy, and the framerate does not always cooperate when things get busy.

I think the soundtrack situation alone makes people opinionated: it’s different, and “different” means fights in comment sections. Some levels hit the spooky tone, others feel like you’re navigating a brown soup with monsters in it—which is honestly kind of authentic to late-90s 3D. Multiplayer deathmatch is the reason to boot it up today, at least for me; solo is fine, but it’s not the main event unless you’re nostalgic for the campaign pacing.

There’s also something funny about playing a PC-native FPS on a controller with a single analog stick in the middle of your life—it rewires your brain in a very specific way. If you can adapt, it’s satisfying; if you can’t, it’s a museum exhibit you visit once. I respect the port for existing at all, because it helped normalize shooters on consoles even when it wasn’t perfect.

Lighting and atmosphere still hit sometimes—Quake’s grim industrial horror survives even when textures are muddy and the resolution is doing its best.

Enemy encounters can feel chunky and abrupt in a very 90s way—less “cinematic,” more “thing in your face now”—which is either spooky or dated depending on your mood. Final thought: one thumb up—important port, fun in bursts, but I wouldn’t call it the definitive way to play Quake in 2026.

**Verdict:** 👍 (one thumb up)
