International Superstar Soccer '98 (ISS 98)
============================================

Description
-----------
Konami soccer sim timed around the 1998 World Cup atmosphere, with national teams, multiple modes (cup, league, scenarios, etc.), and refined gameplay vs earlier N64 ISS entries. Not FIFA-licensed but aimed at realistic-ish arcade/sim hybrid play.

Genre: Sports (association football / soccer)

Developer: Konami Computer Entertainment Osaka

Publisher: Konami

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)
----------------------
• Japan: June 4, 1998  
• North America: August 21, 1998  
• Europe: September 1, 1998  

Ratings / certification
-------------------------
• ESRB: E (Everyone) — US  

Other notes
-----------
• Often reviewed very favorably on N64 for gameplay depth.  
• Known as *Jikkyō J-League Perfect Striker* lineage in Japan for some regional branding variants—verify label for your cart.

Thoughts
--------
ISS 98 is the soccer game I reach for when I want “arcade heart, sim-ish brain” on the N64. It’s not FIFA-licensed, so yeah, the names can look like someone sneezed on a roster sheet, but once you’re actually playing, the fluidity is the argument. Passing strings together feels snappy, shots have weight, and goalkeepers can pull off saves that make you yell at the TV like it’s real life. Highlights: the World Cup-era vibe is baked in even if it’s not “official,” and the modes give you plenty to chew on if you’re the kind of person who starts a tournament and suddenly it’s midnight. Lowlights: if you need real kits and perfect authenticity, you’ll nitpick forever—that’s not what this is selling. Also, learning the quirks takes time; early matches can feel like you’re fighting the ball as much as the opponent.

What I love is how it rewards patience without feeling slow. You can play frantic kick-and-rush football, but the better moments are when you calm down, probe the defense, and exploit a lane you didn’t see ten seconds ago. Multiplayer is glorious chaos—sliding tackles flying, accidental own goals, the usual. Single-player can feel a bit samey if you’re not self-motivated to chase harder difficulties, but that’s sports games in general. Compared to other N64 sports titles, this one still feels “premium” in the controls department, which is the part that actually matters when you’re holding a trident-shaped controller like it owes you money.

There’s also a certain comedy to the unlicensed names—like you’re playing in an alternate universe where spelling is optional—yet the teams still read “real” enough for your brain to buy in. Weather, stadium vibes, and match flow all contribute to that World Cup fantasy even when the graphics are doing their best impression of mashed potatoes. I keep coming back because it feels fair more often than not, and when you score a screamer, you feel like you did it on purpose, not because RNG blessed you. Final thought: if you like soccer even a little, this is one of the system’s best arguments for the genre.

**Verdict:** 👍👍 (two thumbs up)
