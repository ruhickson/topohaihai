Super Smash Bros.

Description

Crossover fighting game where Nintendo characters (Mario, Link, Pikachu, etc.) battle on stages with items and ring-outs. Simpler controls than traditional fighters; spawned a major franchise. First game in the Super Smash Bros. series.

Genre: Fighting / party brawler

Developer: HAL Laboratory

Publisher: Nintendo

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)

• Japan: January 21, 1999
• North America: April 26, 1999
• Europe: November 19, 1999

Ratings / certification

• ESRB: E (Everyone) — US

Other notes

• Small roster compared to later entries; unlockable characters include Jigglypuff and Captain Falcon.
• Sequels on every subsequent Nintendo home console.

Thoughts

The original Smash is almost quaint now—tiny roster, simple stages, and a single-player mode that feels like a fever dream—but it’s also the proof that Nintendo characters could fight without pretending to be Street Fighter. Highlights: it’s easy to pick up, hard to put down, and the “percentage” knockback system still feels clever. Items are chaos, and the sound effects are burned into my brain. Lowlights: it’s not balanced like modern Smash, some characters are clearly better in casual play, and the content is thin compared to Melee and beyond.

What I love is the couch energy: four people, one TV, instant arguments about who picked Kirby. It’s also a museum piece of late-90s Nintendo—Mario, DK, Link, Pikachu, all in one box, which felt impossible at the time. If you go back after playing Ultimate, it feels slow and simple, but that simplicity is part of the charm.

The single-player board-map is bizarre in a lovable way—like Nintendo said “what if fighting game, but also fever dream?” Break the targets, platforming challenges, it’s not just versus modes. It’s a time capsule of ideas before the series became a competitive institution.

Items on can turn any match into nonsense, and for casual play that’s perfect—skilled players might roll their eyes, but that’s what later entries are for. This one is basically “party fight simulator,” and it owns that identity.

Character quirks are simpler than later games, which makes reads easier for beginners—there’s less “move encyclopedia,” more “I jumped wrong and died at 40%.”

Unlocking stuff felt like a secret club back in the day—another reason to keep the N64 warm over a weekend.

Even now, there’s something cozy about how small the roster feels—every match is a reunion, not a spreadsheet. Final thought: two thumbs up—historically massive, still fun for quick sessions, even if later games overshadow it.

Verdict: 👍👍 (two thumbs up)
