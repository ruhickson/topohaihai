Mario Tennis
============

Description
-----------
Arcade tennis with Mario universe characters, courts, and power shots—developed by Camelot (Golden Sun / Mario Golf lineage). Includes singles/doubles, tournaments, and unlockable elements. Introduced Waluigi as a new character in the Mario franchise.

Genre: Sports (tennis)

Developer: Camelot Software Planning

Publisher: Nintendo

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)
----------------------
• Japan: July 21, 2000  
• North America: August 28, 2000  
• Europe: November 3, 2000  

Ratings / certification
-------------------------
• ESRB: E (Everyone) — US  

Other notes
-----------
• GBC companion version (*Mario Tennis GB*) links via Transfer Pak for some features.  
• Follow-up *Mario Power Tennis* on GameCube.

Thoughts
--------
Mario Tennis on N64 is one of those Camelot sports games where the controls feel *right* in a way that’s hard to explain until you’ve served an ace and done a little internal victory lap. It’s fast, readable, and full of Mario character nonsense without turning into pure party randomness—there’s still skill. Highlights: doubles is where it sings, especially if you’ve got a friend who understands positioning and not just mashing power shots. Waluigi’s whole existence basically starts here, which is either a gift or a curse depending on your tolerance for tall purple men. Lowlights: single-player can feel a bit thin after you unlock stuff, and if you’re not into tennis as a sport, the novelty might wear off faster than Mario Golf’s chill vibes.

I love how expressive the animations are for the era—characters feel like themselves, not just reskins with the same swing. The courts add variety without being overwhelming, and the difficulty curve is mostly fair until you hit the cheap AI shots that make you swear the ball has homing. Transfer Pak stuff is cool if you had the hardware; if not, you’re still fine. Compared to other multiplayer staples on the system, this one feels underrated because Mario Kart and Smash steal the oxygen.

Doubles positioning is where the game sneaks in depth: covering the court, baiting shots, setting up smash opportunities. It’s easy to learn the buttons, harder to learn spacing—classic Camelot. I also appreciate that it doesn’t rely on random items as a crutch; the chaos comes from player decisions and timing. If you’re solo, unlocking stuff can feel like a grind, but the core loop stays snappy.

I’ll admit some characters feel stronger in casual play—looking at you, power-shot nonsense—but that’s part of the playground energy. The courts’ gimmicks are mild enough that they add flavor without ruining fairness, which is harder than it sounds. Final thought: two thumbs up—tight gameplay, big personality, great couch sessions.

**Verdict:** 👍👍 (two thumbs up)
