Blast Corps
===========

Description
-----------
Action/puzzle game where you clear a path for a runaway nuclear missile carrier using bulldozers, mechs, and other vehicles to demolish buildings and obstacles. Missions are time-pressured and reward efficient destruction routes.

Genre: Action / puzzle / vehicle combat

Developer: Rare

Publisher: Nintendo

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)
----------------------
• North America: February 28, 1997  
• Japan: August 1, 1997  
• Europe: March 1, 1997  

Ratings / certification
-------------------------
• ESRB: E (Everyone) — US  

Other notes
-----------
• Praised for level design and variety; often cited as an underrated N64 title.

Thoughts
--------
Blast Corps is the kind of game where you start playing “one level” at midnight and suddenly it’s 2am and you’re optimizing bulldozer routes like you’re speedrunning FedEx. The premise is so stupid-good: make a path for a truck that refuses to stop, and if you fail, boom, nuclear drama. Highlights: every vehicle handles differently enough that it never feels like one-note demolition—sometimes you’re precision-surgical, sometimes you’re just chaos. Some levels are basically puzzles where the solution is “what if I used the wrong tool on purpose,” and when it works you feel like a genius. Lowlights: the difficulty spikes can be rude out of nowhere, especially when the timer is breathing down your neck and you’re one misplaced girder away from a restart. Also some objectives are trial-and-error until you internalize the map, which can frustrate if you’re not in a patient mood.

I love how it makes destruction feel like engineering. You’re not just smashing—you’re reading a space and deciding the order of operations. That’s satisfying in a way that pure “rampage” games sometimes miss. The flip side is that the game can be *mean*: you’ll fail a mission and immediately know it was your fault, but you’ll still want to throw the controller because the truck is so unrelenting. It’s also not a looker by modern standards; it’s chunky geometry and explosions that look like confetti, but the clarity helps gameplay. Multiplayer isn’t really the point here, so if you want party vibes, this ain’t it—this is “coffee and focus” gaming.

There’s also a weird emotional arc: early levels make you feel powerful, mid-game introduces gadgets that change your mental model, and late-game asks you to combine everything under pressure. That progression is great, but it can leave you drained if you’re playing late at night—your brain is doing logistics while your eyes are doing N64 blur. Still, when you finally clear a stage that bullied you for an hour, the relief is disproportionate, like you paid off a tiny debt to the universe. Final thought: underrated is correct; it’s crunchy, clever, and weirdly stressful in a satisfying way.

**Verdict:** 👍👍 (two thumbs up)
