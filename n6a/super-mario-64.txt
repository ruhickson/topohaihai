Super Mario 64
==============

Description
-----------
Landmark 3D platformer: Mario explores Princess Peach’s castle hub and jumps into paintings to reach fifteen main courses (plus secrets) collecting Power Stars. Defined the camera, movement, and level design vocabulary for 3D platform games on consoles.

Genre: 3D platformer / action-adventure

Developer: Nintendo EAD

Publisher: Nintendo

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)
----------------------
• Japan: June 23, 1996  
• North America: September 29, 1996  
• Europe/Australia: March 1, 1997  

Ratings / certification
-------------------------
• ESRB: E (Everyone) — US  

Other notes
-----------
• Rereleased on DS as *Super Mario 64 DS* (2004).  
• Part of *Super Mario 3D All-Stars* on Switch (limited release).  
• Nintendo Switch Online + Expansion Pack includes N64 emulation version.

Thoughts
--------
Super Mario 64 is the game that taught a generation how to move in 3D—jump, dive, long jump, wall-kick, and occasionally blame the camera for your own incompetence. Peach’s castle as a hub is still genius: you’re not grinding a menu, you’re exploring a place that hides secrets in plain sight. Highlights: movement is so expressive it basically *is* the game; stars feel earned; Bowser fights are simple but iconic. Lowlights: some stars are annoying to grab with older camera tech, and a few missions are basically “do a thing you already did but harder,” which can feel padded if you’re a completionist hunting 120.

What blows my mind replaying it is how confident it feels: every world has a distinct identity, and Mario’s animations sell weight and playfulness at the same time. It’s not as stuffed with content as modern Mario games, but the sandbox is tight enough that you don’t care as much. I still smile at the first time you triple jump into a painting and the world just *opens*—pure magic.

Speedrun culture basically turned this game into a competitive sport, and playing casually still benefits from that DNA: movement tech is so expressive that you can entertain yourself without ever “finishing” the game. Bowser fights are simple but readable, and the castle’s secret stars reward curiosity without forcing guides—unless you’re hunting 120, in which case you’ll probably consult a map like the rest of us.

I still get lost in the little moments: penguin races, slide times, random stars that feel like secrets even when millions know them. That’s Mario magic—you’re not always chasing the end, you’re chasing vibes.

Even the “bad” camera moments are part of the muscle memory now—like yeah, it’s janky, but it’s *our* janky. Final thought: two thumbs up, obviously—foundational, joyful, and still a joy to speedrun or just mess around in.

**Verdict:** 👍👍 (two thumbs up)
