Castlevania (Castlevania 64)
============================

Description
-----------
The first 3D entry in Konami’s Castlevania series on a home console. Play as Reinhardt Schneider (whip) or Carrie Fernandez (magic) through a 3D Dracula’s castle with branching paths, puzzles, and bosses. A follow-up/expansion-style release, *Castlevania: Legacy of Darkness*, arrived later on N64.

Genre: Action-adventure / hack-and-slash (3D)

Developer: Konami Computer Entertainment Kobe

Publisher: Konami

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)
----------------------
• North America: January 26, 1999  
• Japan: March 11, 1999  
• Europe: May 14, 1999  

Ratings / certification
-------------------------
• ESRB: T (Teen) — US (typical)  

Other notes
-----------
• Often referred to as “Castlevania 64” by fans to distinguish from 2D classics.  
• Two playable characters; different stages/boss order in parts of the game.

Thoughts
--------
I wanted to love this so badly because “Castlevania but 3D” sounded like a dream in ’99, and there are moments where the atmosphere absolutely delivers—like yeah, this is a miserable castle, the sky is wrong, and something’s about to jump out. But playing it now? Oof, the camera fights you like it’s a second boss, and platforming over insta-death pits with janky angles is where my patience taps out. Highlights: the music still hits that gothic spot, some set-pieces are genuinely creepy for the era, and playing Carrie vs Reinhardt does give you a reason to replay. Lowlights: stiff combat, enemies that feel spongy, and those moments where you’re not sure if you missed a jump or the game just didn’t let you see the edge.

It’s weird to admit, but I respect the *attempt* more than the comfort. I kept playing because I wanted the castle to feel like a place, and sometimes it does—tight corridors, weird lighting, that classic “you’re not welcome here” energy. Then you’ll fight a normal enemy in a tight room and the camera will do something rude and you’ll lose health in a way that feels cheap. That’s the loop: curiosity, dread, then mild annoyance. If you’re a Castlevania fan, you’ll still find bits to love; if you’re a casual player, you might bounce hard. I’m not mad I played it, but I’m not racing to replay it.

Also, inventory juggling and pacing can feel old-school in the “not in a cute way” sense—save points and level flow sometimes make you redo chunks because you got clipped by something you barely saw. That’s era-appropriate, but it doesn’t make it funnier in the moment. I will say the two-character setup gives the game more replay value than a straight linear campaign would’ve had, so if you’re determined, there’s meat on the bone. Final thought: worth experiencing as a time capsule, but I wouldn’t hand it to a friend expecting smooth modern vibes—more like “historical curiosity with spikes.”

**Verdict:** 👎 (one thumb down)
