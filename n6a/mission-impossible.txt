Mission: Impossible (N64)
=========================

Description
-----------
Third-person action/infiltration game based on the 1996 *Mission: Impossible* film. Players complete missions as Ethan Hunt with disguises, gadgets, and shooting/stealth sequences spanning multiple international locations.

Genre: Third-person action / stealth

Developer: Infogrames Studios (Bristol) — attribution varies slightly by source; Infogrames-led N64 development

Publisher: Infogrames (NA/EU branding); Ocean Software cited on some regional releases

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)
----------------------
• North America: July 31, 1998  
• Europe: typically 1998 (often cited September 1998 for EU)  

Ratings / certification
-------------------------
• ESRB: T (Teen) — US  

Other notes
-----------
• Often noted as rough compared to contemporary film tie-ins; preservation interest for licensed N64 era.  
• Verify exact EU date on your regional box—1998 releases sometimes differ by a few weeks.

Thoughts
--------
I tried to go into Mission: Impossible on N64 with an open mind—like, maybe it’s secretly a cool stealth sandbox—but honestly it felt like wrestling a shopping cart through molasses. The idea is great on paper: disguises, gadgets, Ethan Hunt doing spy stuff. In practice, highlights are rare flashes where a level clicks and you feel like you’re sneaking for real instead of fighting the camera. Lowlights: the controls feel clumsy, combat is awkward, and objectives are often unclear enough that you’re not “thinking like a spy,” you’re “walking around hoping something triggers.” It’s also one of those licensed games where you can sense the deadline breathing down its neck.

I’m not saying there’s zero fun—some people love the jank and treat it like a B-movie experience—but you have to be in a specific mood: patient, nostalgic, and willing to laugh when something breaks. Graphically it’s very N64, which is fine, but the framerate and movement don’t do the stealth fantasy any favors. Compared to GoldenEye on the same console, it’s painful how wide the quality gap feels.

There are moments where the disguise idea almost works—blending in, walking past guards—and then you’ll clip on geometry or fail a mission because the game didn’t communicate an objective clearly enough. That’s the worst combo: not hard-fair, just confusing. I kept hoping it would “click” into a satisfying stealth loop, and for me it never really did; it felt like work. Your mileage may vary if you enjoy broken games ironically.

I even tried to roleplay it—slow walks, “immersive” stealth—and the game still fought me in basic ways like aiming or reading rooms. That’s when I knew it wasn’t “hardcore,” it was just rough. Final thought: two thumbs down for me personally—it’s more interesting as a relic than as a game I’d recommend for a good time.

**Verdict:** 👎👎 (two thumbs down)
