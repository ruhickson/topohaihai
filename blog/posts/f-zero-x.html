F-Zero X

Description

High-speed futuristic racing sequel to the SNES F-Zero. Features a large roster of pilots and machines, track loops/loops, and a smooth 60 FPS target (noted for its time on N64). Includes Grand Prix, Time Attack, Death Race mode, and multiplayer.

Genre: Racing (futuristic arcade)

Developer: Nintendo EAD

Publisher: Nintendo

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)

• Japan: July 14, 1998
• North America: September 30, 1998
• Europe: November 6, 1998

Ratings / certification

• ESRB: E (Everyone) — US

Other notes

• Expansion Pak used for some features in later bundles/versions (e.g. 64DD-related content in Japan).
• F-Zero X Expansion Kit (Japan, 64DD) added tracks/cars.

Thoughts

F-Zero X is the kind of game that doesn’t ask you to “get good” politely—it launches you into a pipe at Mach ridiculous and expects you to figure out your own dignity. The first time I cleared a cup without flying off the track every thirty seconds, I felt like I’d earned a pilot’s license in real life. Highlights: speed, speed, and also speed, plus that weirdly hypnotic soundtrack that makes you lean into turns like a lunatic on the couch. The roster of pilots gives the game personality even when you’re mostly staring at a tunnel of neon. Lowlights: if you’re not into memorizing tracks, it can feel brutal—this isn’t Mario Kart where items bail you out; it’s you versus physics and your own bad reflexes. Some tracks are basically “if you blink you’re paste,” and the learning curve is steep enough that casual friends might bounce after two races.

What I love is how clean it feels for an N64 racer—like Nintendo chose framerate and responsiveness over flashy textures, and it paid off in the moment-to-moment feel. Death Race mode is a weird little snack: stupid fun for a bit, then you move on. Multiplayer split-screen is chaos in the best way—four people screaming because someone tapped them into oblivion. The downside is content hunger: you can burn through cups, get fast times, and still want “more game” compared to modern racers stuffed with unlockables. Also, visually it’s not going to impress anyone who didn’t grow up with it; it’s mostly geometry and speed lines, which I personally find charming but I get why it looks austere now. I keep thinking about how different it feels from Mario Kart: there’s less “party,” more “you signed up for Gs and consequences.” If you’re the kind of player who likes drilling a track until your hands memorize every micro-turn, you’ll get a lot out of it; if you want pickups and comeback mechanics, you’ll feel abandoned—and that’s kind of the point. Night sessions with friends devolve into laughing rage-quits, then immediate rematches, which is the healthiest toxic cycle in gaming.

Replay value for me is mostly self-imposed: shaving hundredths, learning strats, chasing clean laps. It doesn’t hand you a cinematic story; it hands you a machine and a track and says go. That purity is either refreshing or empty depending on your mood, but I usually land on refreshing. Final thought: if you want a futuristic racer with attitude and zero patience for your mistakes, this still slaps—pure arcade adrenaline.

Verdict: 👍👍 (two thumbs up)
