Paper Mario

Description

RPG starring Mario in a papercraft-inspired world: turn-based battles with timed button boosts, light puzzle exploration, and chapter-based story against Bowser and new partners. First in the Paper Mario series (after Mario RPG on SNES in spirit).

Genre: Role-playing game (RPG)

Developer: Intelligent Systems

Publisher: Nintendo

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)

• Japan: August 11, 2000 (as Mario Story)
• North America: February 5, 2001
• Europe: October 5, 2001

Ratings / certification

• ESRB: E (Everyone) — US

Other notes

• Re-released on Wii Virtual Console and later Nintendo Switch Online + Expansion Pack.
• Distinct art style and humor became series trademarks.

Thoughts

Paper Mario is the RPG I recommend to people who think they don’t like RPGs, because it’s basically a comedy sketch show with turn-based battles. The paper aesthetic isn’t just cute—it’s a whole design language where jokes hide in the world geometry and character animations. Highlights: timed action commands make battles feel interactive instead of menu-heavy, partners add variety, and the writing has that Intelligent Systems warmth—silly, but not mean-spirited. Lowlights: if you’re grinding-averse, you’ll still do some repetitive fights, and backtracking in certain chapters can feel like padding, especially if you’re hunting every secret badge.

What I love most is how the game respects your time emotionally: it’s not trying to be a grim epic; it’s cozy, witty, and occasionally surprisingly heartfelt. Boss fights have personality beyond “big HP bar,” and the music is sneakily memorable. The downside is N64 storage limits show up in small ways—less text variety than later entries, fewer wild set-pieces than Thousand-Year Door—but that’s comparing it to its own successors. As a first outing for the paper style, it’s shockingly confident.

Partners changing your battle options keeps encounters from feeling like pure repetition, and the badge system gives you build-craft without turning into a spreadsheet simulator. Exploration rewards curiosity with hidden blocks and clever gags—Mario RPG traditions, but with a flatter aesthetic. I still think some chapter pacing wobbles near the end, but the highs outweigh the drag.

Bosses like Huff N. Puff are memorable not because they’re brutally hard, but because the fight has a gimmick you actually have to respect—turn-based design with a little theatricality. I also love how the paper look lets the devs cheat in fun ways: folding, sliding, hiding secrets in plain sight. Final thought: two thumbs up, easy—charming, clever, and still a joy to play if you like turn-based with a light touch.

Verdict: 👍👍 (two thumbs up)
