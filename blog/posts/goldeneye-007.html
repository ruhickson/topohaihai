GoldenEye 007

Description

First-person shooter based on the 1995 James Bond film GoldenEye. Single-player mission campaign with objectives and stealth elements; split-screen multiplayer with numerous weapon and character options. Widely regarded as one of the most influential console FPS games.

Genre: First-person shooter

Developer: Rare

Publisher: Nintendo

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)

• Japan: August 23, 1997
• North America: August 25, 1997
• PAL / Europe: often cited August 25, 1997 (some UK-specific sources list a later 1997 date—check regional packaging)

Ratings / certification

• ESRB: T (Teen) — US

Other notes

• Reimagined as GoldenEye 007 (2010) for Wii/HD platforms by different studios.
• Xbox 360 remaster was developed but not officially released; leaked builds exist in preservation circles.

Thoughts

GoldenEye is one of those games that’s half masterpiece, half friendship liability. Campaign-wise, it’s sneaky in a way console shooters barely were at the time—objectives, silenced pistols, trying to be James Bond even though you’ll probably end up spraying bullets like it’s a warehouse sale. Some levels are etched into my brain forever: Facility layouts, guard routes, that quiet tension before alarms go off. Then other levels are basically “okay, survive the chaos,” which is less elegant but still fun. Highlights: multiplayer is the main event—slappers only, proximity mines, someone picking Oddjob and the room instantly debating house rules. Lowlights: by modern standards the framerate can chug, the controls are an acquired taste if you didn’t grow up with them, and the auto-aim conversation will never die.

What still impresses me is how much personality Rare squeezed out of the hardware—missions feel like scenarios, not just corridors. That said, if you replay it today, you’ll notice jank: weird collision, guards who sometimes have ESP, objectives that are easy to soft-fail if you’re not paying attention. I don’t care—I’m still grateful it exists. It’s also a time capsule of Bond fandom: the music hits, the gadgets feel cool even when they’re janky, and the split-screen trash talk is basically the real game.

I can talk for hours about house rules—no Oddjob, mines only, pistols only—and that’s a sign the multiplayer has legs beyond nostalgia. Single-player has its own cult favorites: people speedrun Facility like it’s a religion, while others remember Train or Cradle with equal reverence. The framerate absolutely collapses sometimes, and yes, the controls are “N64 first,” but the game earns your adaptation. Honestly, half the fun is arguing afterward about whether someone screen-watched in split-screen—an experience modern online can’t replicate. Final thought: historically huge, still ridiculously fun with friends, and absolutely two thumbs up even if it’s not “pretty” anymore.

Verdict: 👍👍 (two thumbs up)
