Star Wars Episode I: Racer
==========================

Description
-----------
High-speed podracing game based on the Boonta Eve Classic sequence from *The Phantom Menace*. Upgrade parts, race circuits on multiple planets, and play two-player split-screen. Known for sense of speed and engine-upgrade loop.

Genre: Racing (sci-fi arcade)

Developer: LucasArts (internal development; port teams for consoles)

Publisher: LucasArts; Nintendo published in Japan on some releases

Platform: Nintendo 64

Release dates (retail)
----------------------
• North America: May 18, 1999  
• Europe: typically July 1999 (often cited July 9, 1999)  
• Japan: October 22, 1999  

Ratings / certification
-------------------------
• ESRB: E (Everyone) — US  

Other notes
-----------
• Re-released on modern platforms (e.g., Switch).  
• Expansion Pak can enable higher-resolution mode on N64 (see box).

Thoughts
--------
Podracing is one of the few things from Episode I that people straight-up admit was cool, and this game understands the assignment: speed, engine upgrades, ridiculous tracks, and that “if you blink you’re wallpaper” energy. Highlights: sense of speed is fantastic for the hardware, upgrading parts is addictive in a good way, and two-player split-screen can get intense. Lowlights: it can be punishing—walls eat runs, and some courses are memorization-heavy until you learn the lines.

I love the pod “feel” more than the movie tie-in wrapper, honestly—the fiction is just an excuse to go fast. Some people bounce off the difficulty spike, especially if they wanted Mario Kart forgiveness. Also, N64 fog and draw distance show their age, but the framerate is usually the bigger concern in tight turns.

The upgrade loop is sneaky: you start thinking “one more race for parts,” and suddenly you’ve optimized a pod like it’s a hobby shop project. Two-player split-screen is where friendships get tested—tiny mistakes become huge disasters at those speeds.

Tracks have personality—some wide and forgiving, others narrow and mean—and the game does a good job teaching you that podracing is basically risk management with explosions. I love the little cockpit details and audio cues that sell speed even when the graphics are doing N64 things.

Repair costs and upgrades create a light RPG loop on top of racing—nothing crazy, but enough to keep you engaged between events. Blowing up feels awful in a fun way because it’s usually your own greed for speed.

Sebulba energy is real: sometimes you’re the hero, sometimes you’re the villain, sometimes you’re both after one bad corner.

I keep coming back for the fantasy of mastering a stupid-fast machine without dying—simple goal, hard execution, addictive loop. Final thought: two thumbs up—one of the better licensed racers on the system, and it still holds up as an arcade adrenaline fix.

**Verdict:** 👍👍 (two thumbs up)
